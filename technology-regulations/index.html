US Technology Regulations, Laws and Policies

Exploring US technology regulations landscape in depth.

In the digital age enormous amount of data is collected and processed pertaining to users who is connected to the internet and uses a digital service, social media and other platforms. If this data ends up in wrong hands or used for purposes other than it is intended for will lead to negative consequences. Examples include privacy concerns, manipulation of users political opinions using targeted news, social engineering and identity theft to name a few. Misuse of data could also pose threat to a nation if sensitive government data is mishandled.

In order to address the concerns surrounding data United States Federal government has passed laws to protect its citizens. These laws regulate data collection, data processing, data usage, data dissemination and data disposal. A firm is penalized if it doesn’t comply with these laws. These laws boil down to Preserving Individual Autonomy and securing National interests.

Children’s Online Privacy Protection Act

  • Enacted in 1998, COPPA aims to protect the privacy and personally identifying information of children under the age of 13 who use online services (Fruhlinger, 2021).
  • Came into being when Center for Media Education compliant about KidsCom to FTC. KidsCom violated many FTC laws on how data collected from kids is mishandled.
  • Designed to protect personal information of children of age 13 and young.
  • Gives control to the parents of the child control over the online collection, use, or disclosure of personal information from children. Parents can determine what information can be shared and how it can be used via parental controls.
  • Online websites cannot collect information that is not clearly stated and should be with the information specified by the act.
  • Websites also cannot passively track children.
  • Information should be deleted from the IT systems upon request.
  • Examples of violations include Google’s multi million dollar settlement with FTC.

Gramm-Leach-Bliley Act

  • The law was enacted in 1999.
  • It is also called Financial Services Modernization Act of 1999. It was enacted to protect non-public consumer data from misuse.
  • All Financial institutions, brokers, dealers, and people providing insurance services, including investment companies and investment advisors should be in complianc (Liu, nd).
  • Important clauses include.
    • Ensure the security and confidentiality of customer records and information
    • Protect from threats and maintain integrity of customer information.
    • Protect customer information from unauthorized access.
    • Express consent has to be obtained if data is used for any other purpose than clearly stated.

Telephone Consumer Protection Act of 1991

  • The TCPA was enacted in 1991.
  • It protects individuals from Tele marketing calls without their consent.
  • The law was enacted as a response to a growing rise in unregulated and harassing telemarketing calls and faxes.
  • Fines for violation range from 500 USD to 1500 USD.
  • Any business, person, or entity that conducts telephone solicitations should comply with the TCPA.
  • The TCPA covers voice calls, faxes, VoIP calls, and text messages.
  • Express written consent has to be obtained from consumers before calling(Hayes, 2022).

Fair Credit Reporting Act

  • FCRA was enacted in 1970 to promote accuracy, fairness, and the privacy of personal information assembled by Credit Reporting Agencies (CRAs).
  • Consumers have rights provided by this act (Kagan, 2023).
    • Verify accuracy of their credit report.
    • Receive notification if information on their file is used to deny credit applications.
    • Dispute and request for correction of credit information on their file.
    • Remove outdated and negative information after seven years in most cases, or 10 in the case of some bankruptcies
    • File compliant with Consumer Financial Protection Bureau in case of in action by Credit Reporting agencies.
  • Both Federal Trade Commission (FTC) and the Consumer Financial Protection Bureau (CFPB) are responsible for enforcing FCRA.

Health Insurance Portability and Accountability Act

  • Enacted in The Health 1996 , Health Insurance Portability and Accountability Act of (HIPAA) is a federal law that required the creation of national standards to protect sensitive patient health information from being disclosed without the patient’s consent or knowledge.
  • Since patient records are digitally stored including Computerized Physician order entry systems, Electronic Health Records, radiology, pharmacy, and laboratory systems. Patient data is moving between the systems for convenience which also increases security issues.
  • There are physical and technical safeguards enforced by HIPAA (De Groot, 2023)
  • Authorized access to physical data and limited access to data.
  • Access policies at workplaces.
  • Restrictions on movement of data.
  • Data must be encrypted.
  • Auditing has to be performed during data access.
  • Violations results in penalties ranging from $100 - $25000
